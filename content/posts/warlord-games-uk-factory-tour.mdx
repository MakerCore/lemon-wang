I spent last week at UKGE and TCT 3Sixty. Thousands of people, hundreds of booths, the usual.

Then on Friday I drove to Nottingham, paid £15 at the door, and walked into a factory that still makes toy soldiers by hand. I stayed four hours. I recorded everything.

---

Warlord Games. 2007, founded by John Stallard and Paul Sawyer. Both laid off from Games Workshop. Both used their redundancy money to make the first box of plastic Roman soldiers.

Today: ~110 people. Bolt Action does about 40% of revenue. 1,100 dealers. And under one roof, four completely different manufacturing processes.

**They haven't automated the factory. They did the math and chose not to.**

---

## The man at the bench

Alexander. Traditional resin. Silicone moulds, a measuring cup, two-part epoxy, tinted grey to match the Games Workshop standard set 25 years ago.

15 trays a day by hand. 23 with a new injection machine. 6,000 mould cavities on his best days.

The mould costs £100–1,000. A steel injection tool starts at £20,000.

The Italian operations manager said it flat:

> *"We know if we make it in plastic we'll never get the money back. But in resin we can have the elephant."*

The elephant is every obscure German halftrack, every niche historical unit no injection mould would ever justify. Alexander's bench is not a legacy process they forgot to replace. It's a deliberately maintained capability that makes their entire catalogue viable.

---

## Two days from design to sale

Another room: centrifugal casting, same process as jewellery. A metal mould costs a few hundred pounds. Time from finished design to sellable product: two days.

The metal team lead:

> *"There is an advantage to metal that nothing else has — we can bring a product to market very quickly."*

This is their market validation engine. Try it in metal. If it moves, graduate to resin or plastic. If it flops, the loss doesn't matter.

Alexander again:

> *"If you live in America, you probably got something I made in the last two days."*

Not hyperbole. Almost every metal wargaming figure sold globally comes through this building.

---

| Process | Tooling | Output | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional resin | £100–1,000 | Low, manual | Long-tail SKUs |
| Warlord Resin | Low (silicone) | ~4,000/day | Mid-volume flex |
| Metal centrifugal | Few hundred | 100–200/day/person | Market validation |
| Injection moulding | £20,000+ | Very high | Flagship products |

Four processes, one logic. Expensive tooling for the mass-market products. Cheap, manual processes for everything else. The niche, the experimental, the long tail that turns a single game into a complete universe.

---

## Why the birthplace of the Industrial Revolution still pours resin by hand

Britain taught the world to replace hands with machines. Then I walked into Warlord Towers and watched a man at a bench, twenty years, same process.

This is not stubbornness. This is not "craftsmanship" — not the sentimental kind.

This is cold, calculated business logic.

The Chinese manufacturing mindset I come from runs on scale: maximise throughput, drive marginal cost to zero. It's powerful, and it's not wrong. But it has a blind spot.

When demand is too small, too niche, too weird, the scale logic abandons it. Not because the demand isn't real. Because it doesn't fit the model.

Warlord inverted that: **because silicone moulds and hand-casting are cheap, we can serve demand no injection line would touch.**

They can explain it — cost of a mould, cavities per tray, lifetime of a silicone form.

That's what got me. Not the craft. The clarity.

---

## What I didn't see coming

Halfway through the tour, I showed the Italian manager what a full-colour MJP printer can do. CMYK + white + clear. Water-soluble supports. Models out of the machine, painted.

His face changed before he said anything:

> *"So basically 3D printer with the color already, no paint, no any finish processing?"*

Not polite. Genuine. He had never heard of this applied to miniatures.

He pulled in their most 3D-printing-savvy engineer. The engineer looked at the samples:

> *"That's pretty cool."*

That was it. No objections. No follow-up. No business inquiry.

This is not rejection. It's a knowledge gap. They're not skeptical — they're uninformed.

Here's why it matters: every long-tail SKU on Alexander's bench — every unit that only exists because silicone moulds are cheap — could be produced with zero tooling, in full colour, on demand.

No mould, no casting, no separate painting.

The entry point isn't replacing Bolt Action infantry sprues. It's replacing the traditional resin line where the math already works.

---

## What I didn't see coming

John Stallard told me directly:

> *"In 5-10 years, every man and woman will have one of those in their kitchen, but not yet."*

He's right about the direction. I'm less sure about the timeline.

They already released an STL this April. Not because they wanted to. Traditional manufacturing cost pushed them there. They already use 3D printing for design prototypes.

What they haven't considered is 3D printing as a production method for finished, coloured product. Not files. Not prototypes. Product.

A £15 factory tour won't close that gap. But a few more people walking through that door with samples in their pocket might.
