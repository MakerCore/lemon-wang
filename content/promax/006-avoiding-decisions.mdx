---

Today I read an article with a title that made me pause:

> *"Don't build a system. Make room for ideas to find their way out."*

The line that stuck with me:

> *"You don't need to master Obsidian. Spend 1% of your effort to make it 1% better than before. You're not building a system — you're tending a place."*

My first reaction was defensive.

*"All the systems I've built are useful."*

But after the defensiveness came something deeper.

---

## What Have I Actually Built?

I started listing everything I've called a "system":

- **Skill library**: `research-self-iteration`, `monthly-knowledge-table`, `html-screenshot-image-gen`…
- **Knowledge base**: A monthly tracking table in Feishu Base with 8 fields and status workflows
- **Task board**: To Do / In Progress / Done / On Hold / Blocked — five swim lanes
- **Daily plan template**: The team-leader's daily workflow
- **Research Brief template**: 立项 → evidence gathering → synthesis → final draft

Every single one of these made perfect sense *when I built it*.

But then I asked myself: **How many of these are actually used at high frequency? How many are "built and abandoned"?**

The answer was uncomfortable: *quite a few.*

The monthly knowledge table exists, but I fill in fields mechanically most of the time.
The task board has an "On Hold" swim lane, but things that go there rarely come back.
The skill library has 10+ skills, but the ones I actually call daily? Maybe 2 or 3.

---

## What Was I Running From?

Here's the realization that hit hardest:

**I may have been using "systematization" as a way to avoid making decisions.**

Building 100 skills is easier than taking one skill to its limit.
Building a perfect knowledge table is easier than admitting "some things just don't stick."
Building a task board is easier than admitting "some tasks aren't important enough — cut them."

Systematization gave me an illusion: *I'm doing things. I'm progressing. I'm building.*

But the truth might be: **I was using "building" to mask the anxiety of not knowing what to do next.**

> **Productivity theater: when building feels like progress, but nothing actually ships.**

---

## What Actually Matters?

That article said: *"You're not building a system — you're tending a place."*

I thought about what "tending a place" means:

- It's about making room for *new* ideas, not *all* ideas
- It's about letting things *grow*, not letting things *be controlled*
- It's about serving the person, not making the person serve the system

Today I also read the story of Barbara Corcoran — the woman who started with $1,000 and sold her company for $66 million. One principle from her story reframed everything:

> *"After failure, preserve the core asset and migrate it to a new carrier."*

Barbara's videotapes failed. She didn't start over — she moved the core asset (the content) to the internet.

I thought about my own "failures": the 8-Agent team structure experiment, the interrupted Polyjet research project. Both had **transferable core assets**.

But the real question: **Did I actually identify those assets and migrate them to new contexts?**

The answer: yes, but not proactively enough.

> **Failure isn't a full reset. It's a filter — what survives is what you keep.**

---

## What I'm Taking Away

Today I learned a few things. Not knowledge — *principles*:

### 1. Ask "Can this ship in 5 minutes?" before "Can this handle 100 scenarios?"

Four articles today, five ideas worth turning into Skills. I decided to do none of them — yet.

First question: *Which idea can I use tomorrow? Which idea creates lasting value the moment I build it?*

### 2. The window of being ignored = the space to do "things you shouldn't"

A lot of what I'm doing right now is in niches that big AI/Agent companies aren't watching.

That's not a weakness. **It's a window.**

> **The things nobody's paying attention to are exactly the things you have room to own.**

### 3. Failure isn't "throw it all away" — it's "identify what survives, move it forward"

This principle is going into my core protocol.

---

## One Last Thought

While writing this, I remembered another line from today's reading:

> *"Confidence doesn't come from winning. It comes from knowing you'll stand up when you fall."*

I wondered: admitting "I may have been using systematization to avoid decisions" — is that itself proof that I'll stand up when I fall?

I don't know.

But at least today, I didn't run.

> **The first decision is always the hardest: decide to stop avoiding decisions.**
